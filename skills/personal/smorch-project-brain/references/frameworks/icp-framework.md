# ICP Framework — Pain Hierarchy, Persona Construction, Buying Behavior

## PURPOSE
Use this when generating icp.md. Ensures the ICP is specific enough to target, rich enough to message, and validated enough to trust.

---

## PERSONA CONSTRUCTION (4 layers)

### Layer 1: Demographics (The WHO)
The LinkedIn-searchable identity.

**Required fields:**
- Name: Fictional but specific ("Khalid," not "Marketing Manager")
- Role: Job title + seniority level
- Company: Type (SaaS startup / enterprise / agency), size (employees), revenue range
- Industry: Specific vertical, not "technology"
- Geography: City, not just country. Dubai ≠ Abu Dhabi ≠ Riyadh
- Defining characteristic: One sentence that makes them findable on LinkedIn Sales Navigator

**Test:** Can you build a Sales Navigator search that returns 50-500 results matching this persona? If yes, demographics are specific enough. If it returns 50,000, too broad. If 5, too narrow.

### Layer 2: Psychographics (The WHY)
What drives their decisions beyond logic.

**Required fields:**
- Day-in-the-life: 3-4 sentences describing a typical workday. Name the tools they open, the meetings they attend, the frustrations they hit by 11am
- Goals: What they're trying to achieve this quarter (professional, not personal)
- Fears: What keeps them from taking action (risk aversion, past failures, political risk within company)
- Identity: How they see themselves ("I'm the person who always finds the deal" or "I'm the fixer who inherited this mess")
- Status: What would make them look good to their boss/board

**Test:** Does the psychographic profile explain WHY they would buy, not just WHAT they would buy? If you can swap in a different product and the psychographic still works, it's too generic.

### Layer 3: Pain Statements (The WHAT)
The specific, quotable pains that drive purchase decisions.

**Pain Hierarchy (5 levels, from surface to existential):**

| Level | Type | Example | Messaging Value |
|-------|------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Inconvenience | "I waste 2 hours/week updating spreadsheets" | Low — everyone has inconveniences |
| 2 | Productivity loss | "My team spends 30% of their time on manual data entry instead of selling" | Medium — quantifiable but common |
| 3 | Revenue impact | "We lose 3 deals/month because we detect buying signals too late" | HIGH — directly tied to money |
| 4 | Competitive threat | "Our competitors are using signal-based outbound and closing deals we don't even know about" | VERY HIGH — fear of being left behind |
| 5 | Existential | "If we don't fix our pipeline generation by Q3, we'll miss our Series B milestone" | CRITICAL — tied to survival |

**Rules for pain statements:**
- Always include at least one Level 3+ pain. Surface-level pains don't drive purchase decisions
- Each pain must be quotable: something the ICP would actually say in a meeting
- Each pain must include: frequency (daily/weekly/monthly) + dollar cost + emotional weight
- Minimum 5 pains for DEEP mode, 3 for QUICK mode
- Pains must be DIVERSE: not 5 variations of the same complaint

**Pain statement format:**
"[Specific situation] causing [quantified consequence] which makes them feel [emotion]"

Example: "Reps discover competitor activity 3 weeks after the fact, losing an estimated $50K/quarter in deals that went to competitors they could have preempted — creates a sense of helplessness and reactive firefighting"

### Layer 4: Buying Behavior (The HOW)
How they discover, evaluate, and purchase solutions.

**Required fields:**
- Discovery channels: Where they learn about new solutions (LinkedIn feed, peer recommendations, events, Google search, WhatsApp groups)
- Evaluation process: How many tools do they compare? Who else is involved? What's their checklist?
- Decision authority: Who says yes? Who can say no? Who influences?
- Budget: What can they approve without committee? What requires sign-off?
- Purchase triggers: What specific event makes them start actively looking? (New quarter targets, competitor move, team expansion, mandate from above)
- Objections: Top 3 reasons they say no, even when they need the solution
- Churn triggers: What would make them leave after buying?

## ANTI-PERSONA (Equally Important)

Define 2-3 types of people who LOOK like your ICP but aren't. This prevents wasting outreach on bad-fit prospects.

**Anti-persona format:**
"[Description] — looks like ICP because [similarity]. Not ICP because [disqualifier]. Cost of pursuing: [wasted time/resources]."

**Examples:**
- "Freelance consultants who run a one-person 'agency' — look like ICP because they're in the same LinkedIn groups and post about similar topics. Not ICP because they can't afford $500/month and won't have the team to implement. Cost: 3 calls before discovering budget mismatch."
- "Enterprise companies with 500+ employees — look like ICP because they have the budget and the problem. Not ICP because their procurement cycle is 6+ months, they require SOC2 compliance we don't have, and the deal size doesn't justify the sales cycle. Cost: 3 months of enterprise sales motions for a maybe."

## VALIDATION CHECKLIST

After generating icp.md, verify:

- [ ] Persona has a name, not just a role
- [ ] Company description is specific enough for a Sales Nav search returning 50-500 results
- [ ] Geography is city-level, not just "MENA"
- [ ] At least 5 pain statements (3 for QUICK mode)
- [ ] At least one pain is Level 3+ (revenue impact or higher)
- [ ] Each pain has frequency + dollar cost + emotional weight
- [ ] Dream outcome is quantified (not just "better results")
- [ ] Buying behavior includes specific discovery channels (not just "online")
- [ ] Budget range is specific (not "varies")
- [ ] At least 2 anti-personas defined (DEEP mode)
- [ ] Access channels are specific and actionable (not just "social media")
